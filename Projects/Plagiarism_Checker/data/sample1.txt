Have you ever stopped to think about what really goes into the food you eat? Many consumers may not realise that much of the food they purchase from the supermarket has been genetically modified. Genetically modified organisms (GMOs) were first introduced in 1973 by two pioneering scientists, Herbert Boyer and Stanley Cohen. By successfully transferring DNA from one bacterium into another, they marked the beginning of genetic engineering. Since then, the advancement of GMOs has allowed crops to become more resistant to pests and diseases while producing higher yields and giving them longer shelf lives. While GMOs have led to more nutritious and appealing fruits and vegetables, should we question the ethical and health implications of genetically modifying the food we eat daily?

When we consider the morality, ecological consequences, and health uncertainties linked to GMOs, it becomes clear that this scientific advancement may come with significant risks. It is important to consider whether the potential benefits of genetic modification outweigh its unknown long-term effects. This concern becomes even more poignant when we consider the application of GMOs to animal farming.

In recent years, GMOs have accelerated agricultural development, as they can save years of research, whereas traditional methods such as selective breeding can take decades. GMOs have also been used to help tackle the malnutrition crises in developing countries. One example is golden rice, a variety engineered to be rich in vitamin A, which has been introduced in countries such as Bangladesh and India to help reduce vitamin deficiencies. While GMOs have some clear advantages, particularly in enhancing animal health and productivity of livestock, their use in animals raises ethical concerns about their well-being. Most genetically modified animals are used for researching genes, how they function, and their connections to health and disease. For example, GMO testing aims to improve animal cells, making them healthier and more beneficial. Although genetic engineering has proven useful, it does not resolve all reproductive issues in livestock. Furthermore, genetically modified animals can sometimes have unexpected health outcomes, which raises concern about their health and welfare. There are also many long-term consequences of altering animal genetics. Genetic diversity is one of the major concerns; when specific traits are selected, other genetic material may be lost. Eventually, this can lead to individuals in a species becoming too genetically similar, which, in turn, can lead to inbreeding. Inbred animals can have many health issues due to the likelihood of having two identical alleles (one from each parent); this is called inbreeding depression, which is very harmful to a species as they become more vulnerable to disease and environmental changes. So, is the advancement of GMOs truly worth it when you consider the practical, moral, and ethical implications?

While scientific research has not shown that GM foods are dangerous, there is a small risk that they could cause an allergic reaction. This risk can only arise if the genetic modification introduces a new allergen or enhances the presence of an existing one. According to a YouGov survey conducted in 2022, only 3% of UK respondents said they would prefer to have GM ingredients in their foods, which suggests that the public is sceptical and cautious about GMOs. Not only is the general public sceptical about GMOs, so too is the scientific community. A professor at the Salk Institute for Biological Studies, Dr. David Schubert, stressed that GM food has uncertain environmental impacts and could affect people’s health in ways that cannot yet be predicted. Since the long-term effects of genetically modified food are not fully understood, should we lean on the side of being more cautious?

There are growing concerns about biodiversity and the potential ecological consequences of releasing GM animals into our environment. One major issue with releasing these animals into the wild is that they may have the potential to thrive in the wild, which could cause native species to be outcompeted and lead to GM animals becoming invasive. This could be highly detrimental, as it is difficult to exterminate invasive species and reverse the damage they cause to the environment, leading to irreversible ecological loss. On the other hand, GM crops offer several ecological advantages over animals. They are more resistant to harsh climates, such as drought, heat, and salty soils. This indicates that GM crops could outlive current plants and reduce stress on farmers’ jobs significantly. Another important factor that must be taken into account is the environmental impact of GM plants, particularly when they change the interactions between plants and the abiotic environment. Since plants are a very vital element of the soil ecosystem and essential to the functioning of global ecological systems, there are rising concerns that GM crops might be harming the soil environment. Ultimately, when introducing new science into such a tight-knit and fundamental relationship, it is crucial to ensure there are no safety hazards. So, do the benefits outweigh the costs?

The current lack of GMO labelling undermines consumer rights and clarity in the food industry. In many countries, products containing GMOs are not required to be labelled, leading to customers being unaware of the presence of GMOs in their food. Considering that GMOs are a relatively new scientific discovery used to modify the genetic makeup of organisms and the long-term effects remain unknown, should customers have the right to know if they are consuming them? Unfortunately, many countries are not required to label GMOs present in foods. An example of this used to be the United States, which did not have specific regulations for GMO labelling. In 2018, a survey found that 88% of US consumers believed that there should be clear labelling on foods containing GMOs. This emphasises the importance of transparency regarding the food we buy. Former president of the United States of America, Barack Obama, pledged that GMO labelling is a fundamental right and that people should be aware of what is in the food they eat. During his 2007 presidential campaign, Barack Obama stated what he would do if elected: “Here's what I'll do as president... We'll let folks know whether their food has been genetically modified because Americans should know what they are buying”. Fortunately, in 2022, the regulations changed and GMOs are now required to be labelled in the US, while Canada, Mexico, and many other countries still go without GMO labelling. Since the majority of people support improved labelling, should changes be made to better protect consumer rights?

I firmly believe that we should postpone the widespread use of GMOs until further research has assessed the potential health and environmental impacts. While the increasing global population is causing a greater need for food security, rushing into GMO use without fully understanding the consequences is potentially dangerous. Introducing GM species into our environment poses risks that we are not yet equipped to manage, given the current gaps in knowledge. GMOs must first be proven to be 100% safe before they become part of our diet and are introduced into our environment. Achieving this level of certainty would give people confidence in both their food and the safety of our environment. For the well-being of worldwide health, our environment, and the maintenance of our ethical standards, we should advocate for more transparency and extensive research into the hazards of GMOs. By prioritising these principles, we can ensure that GMOs serve the best interests of society and our planet.
